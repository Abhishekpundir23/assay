Why your AI passes review, then breaks in front of customers

The demo is the easy 80%. You typed three reasonable questions, got three good answers, and shipped. Then production happens:

  • A customer pastes an article into the chat and the model follows instructions hidden inside it (prompt injection).
  • Someone asks a benign question and the assistant refuses — or answers something it should have escalated.
  • The RAG answer sounds confident and cites a policy that doesn't exist.
  • The agent calls the wrong tool with bad arguments, loops, and never stops.
  • You swap to a cheaper model, every spot-check still passes — and a regression you can't see quietly degrades a quarter of your answers.

"It works when I try it" is not a quality signal — it's the absence of one. That's the gap Assay closes.

The reliability audit — three steps, one scorecard

A documented method, run the same way every time — so results are comparable across runs, versions, and vendors.

STEP 01

Map the failure surface

I map your feature to a curated failure-mode taxonomy — hallucination, retrieval, refusals, tool use, prompt injection, PII leakage, tone, drift — and design a test suite for the modes that matter to your product, not a generic benchmark.

STEP 02

Run, red-team & score

I run your feature through the suite with two layers of scoring: deterministic checks (system-prompt leak? valid JSON? PII? latency?) plus an LLM-as-judge for the judgment calls — including adversarial injection and jailbreak cases.

STEP 03

Deliver the scorecard + fix list

You get an overall grade, a pass rate per failure mode, the exact failing cases with input/output/why, and a prioritized list of what to fix first. Plain enough for a founder, precise enough for the engineer.

Optional retainer: I freeze your suite and re-run it on every model or prompt change — so a swap that silently breaks things gets caught before it ships.

The deliverable: a scorecard you can act on Monday

Not a slide deck of opinions. A concrete, evidence-backed report.

  • An overall reliability grade and pass rate — one honest number for "is this safe to ship."
  • Reliability by failure mode — see where the weakness is, not just that there is one.
  • Top failures, with evidence — the exact input, the model's actual output, and why it failed. No "trust me," just receipts.
  • A prioritized fix list — ranked by impact and severity, so you fix the dangerous and common things first.
  • A ship / no-ship verdict — and, with the retainer, a regression diff between versions.
  • Judge trust, stated honestly — I measure judge-vs-human agreement (Cohen's kappa), so scores aren't a black box.

You keep the report and the test suite — a permanent regression asset for your team.

Questions

What exactly do you audit?
Any LLM-powered feature — a chatbot, a RAG/knowledge assistant, a tool-using agent, or a backend model in a pipeline. I can test a hosted HTTP endpoint, a function in your codebase, or a model directly.
Do I have to give you production access or data?
No production write access, ever. For the free mini-audit I work against your public or staging endpoint with synthetic and adversarial inputs I generate — no customer data required.
Isn't an LLM judging another LLM unreliable?
It is — if you don't check it. I use deterministic checks wherever a question has a hard answer, reserve the LLM judge for genuine judgment calls, and report how much that judge agrees with human labels (Cohen's kappa). A judge nobody validated is worthless; a validated one is leverage.
How is this different from the evals my team already runs?
Most in-house evals are a happy-path set written by the people who built the feature, scored by "looks good to me." Assay brings an outside failure-mode taxonomy, adversarial/red-team cases, a measured judge, and regression monitoring across versions. It's the difference between checking your own homework and an audit.
How long does it take?
The free mini-audit turns around in a few days. A full fixed-scope audit is typically 1–2 weeks depending on failure-mode count and red-team depth — with price and scope agreed before I start.
